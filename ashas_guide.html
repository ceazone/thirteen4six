The Big Picture

What we're doing, why, and where we stand right now.

Our Property

We have a 1-bedroom, 1-bathroom basement suite in Columbia Heights, DC. It has a full kitchen, in-unit laundry, and a private entrance — which is a big deal for guests. It's listed on Airbnb as "Private DC Suite · Free Parking · 9 Min to Metro."

What's Going Great

Our guest experience is exceptional. We have a perfect 5.0 rating across 64 reviews, hit all 4 Superhost criteria, and guests constantly mention cleanliness and communication. The product is not the problem — it's amazing.

What We're Fixing: Pricing

We were charging roughly the same rate ($133–150/night) no matter the day — a random Tuesday in January was priced the same as July 4th weekend. That meant we were leaving thousands of dollars on the table during peak DC events, while also not being competitive enough during slow periods to fill the calendar.

The fix: dynamic pricing through PriceLabs, which automatically adjusts our nightly rate based on demand, day of the week, seasonality, and local events.

Our Goal

Go from ~$1,900/month → $3,800/month within 90 days by getting occupancy from ~40% up to 70%+ and raising our average nightly rate. The DC 1-bedroom median earns about $36K/year — we're targeting $45K/year.

Bottom line: Guests love our place. We just need to charge the right price on the right nights. That's what PriceLabs does, and that's what this whole strategy is built around.

How PriceLabs Works

Think of PriceLabs as a smart assistant that adjusts our price every single day so we don't have to.

What Is PriceLabs?

PriceLabs is a tool (~$20/month) that connects to our Airbnb listing and automatically sets our nightly price based on what's happening in the DC market. It looks at things like: how many other listings are available, what day of the week it is, whether there's a big event in town, and how far out the date is.

Once it's set up, it pushes updated prices to Airbnb automatically — we don't have to log in and change prices manually.

The Key Settings We've Configured

SettingWhat We SetWhat It Means
Base Price $155/night This is our "anchor." PriceLabs adjusts UP or DOWN from this number. The DC average for 1-bedrooms is $146, so we're slightly above average.
Minimum Price $115/night The absolute lowest it will ever go — even on a slow Tuesday in January. This covers our costs.
Maximum Price $400/night The ceiling for once-in-a-lifetime events (like the July 4th 250th anniversary).
Auto-Sync ON PriceLabs pushes prices to Airbnb every day automatically. You don't need to click anything for this.

The Smart Rules (Already Set Up)

These are automatic rules that run in the background. You don't need to manage them — just know they're working:

DC weekends are much busier than weekdays. This rule automatically bumps Friday and Saturday night prices by 35% above the weekday rate. So if a Tuesday is $155, Friday might be $209.

If a night is still empty and it's less than 3 days away, PriceLabs drops the price 15% to try to fill it. An empty night earns $0, so even a discounted booking is better.

People who book 2+ months ahead are planners — they're usually less price-sensitive. This charges them a little more, and we can always drop the price later if the date doesn't fill.

If someone books Mon–Wed and someone else books Fri–Sun, Thursday is "orphaned." This rule drops that lonely night's price by 20% so it actually gets booked instead of sitting empty.

7+ nights: 20% discount — great for tourists on long trips.
28+ nights: 35% discount — targets government interns, contractors, and people relocating to DC. These are our most profitable guests because there's less turnover and cleaning.

If the month is filling up fast (over 70% booked), PriceLabs automatically raises prices on the remaining nights by 5–15%. Simple supply and demand — fewer available nights = charge more.

The One Thing PriceLabs Can't Do Alone

PriceLabs is smart, but it doesn't know about every DC event. For the biggest events (July 4th, Capital Pride, major concerts), we manually set "override" prices — specific prices we lock in so the algorithm doesn't underprice them. These are already set for 2026, but when big events are announced for 2027, we'll need to add new overrides.

This is covered in the Pricing Calendar tab.

Your role with PriceLabs: You don't need to log into PriceLabs every day. It runs on autopilot. Your main job is the monthly check-in (covered in the Monthly Routine tab) to make sure our base price is still right and upcoming events have overrides set.

The Command Center

Our custom dashboard that puts everything in one place — open command_center.html in your browser.

What Is It?

The Command Center is an HTML file that lives on our computer. You open it in Chrome or Safari just like a website. It's a single dashboard that shows you revenue, occupancy, pricing, upcoming bookings, market data, and action items — all in one place, so you don't need to bounce between Airbnb, PriceLabs, and spreadsheets.

It's password-protected (use the code Cesar set up). Once you're in, you'll see several sections:

What You'll See Inside

1

Score Cards (top of page)

Four big numbers at a glance: Revenue (how much we earned this month), Occupancy (what % of nights are booked), ADR (average nightly rate), and Rating. Each card is color-coded — green means on track, yellow means watch it, red means we need to act.

2

Revenue Chart

A month-by-month chart showing our income trend and how it compares to the $3,800/month target. You can quickly see if we're growing or if a month dipped.

3

Event Calendar

Lists upcoming DC events with the price we should be charging. Events with a red left border mean we haven't set an override yet — that's your signal to act. Checkmarks mean the override is already set in PriceLabs.

4

Market Position

Shows how our listing compares to other DC listings — our nightly rate vs. the market median, our rating vs. average, etc. This helps you know if we're pricing too high, too low, or just right.

5

Revenue Simulator

A slider tool where you can play with different ADR and occupancy numbers to see what our monthly income would be. Helpful for thinking about "what if we raised the base price $10?"

6

Quick Links Bar

At the very top, there's a row of buttons that take you directly to Airbnb, PriceLabs, and other tools — so you're never more than one click away from whatever you need.

Important: The Data Doesn't Update Itself

The Command Center is a local HTML file — it doesn't automatically pull data from Airbnb. Once a month, you (or Cesar) need to update the numbers inside the file. This is a quick 5-minute job covered in the Monthly Routine tab.

Cleaning Fees & How They Factor In

The prices in our calendar are the nightly rate only. Here's how cleaning fees work on top of that.

How Guests See It

When a guest searches on Airbnb, they see two things: the nightly rate (what PriceLabs sets) and the cleaning fee (a flat fee added once per stay, no matter how many nights). So a 3-night stay at $155/night + $75 cleaning fee = $540 total before Airbnb's service fees.

This is important because the total price — not just the nightly rate — is what the guest decides on. A high cleaning fee on a short stay can make us look expensive even if the nightly rate is fair.

Our Cleaning Fee Strategy

The cleaning fee is set on Airbnb directly (not in PriceLabs). PriceLabs knows about our cleaning fee and factors it into the total guest price when it sets nightly rates, but it doesn't change the cleaning fee itself.

ApproachRecommendationWhy
Current cleaning fee $75 This is roughly what we're charging now (ranges from $50–$100 in past bookings). $75 is fair for a professional clean of a 1BR.
Short stays (1–2 nights) Keep at $75 The cleaning effort is the same whether someone stays 1 night or 2. Don't discount it. But know that this makes our total price per night higher for short stays — that's fine, it discourages unprofitable 1-night bookings.
Long stays (7+ nights) $75 is fine — it becomes negligible $75 spread over 7 nights = only $10.70/night extra. Over 28 nights it's $2.68/night. The longer the stay, the less the cleaning fee matters.

The Math That Matters: Total Guest Price

Here's what a guest actually pays for different stay lengths at a $155/night rate with a $75 cleaning fee:

Stay LengthNightly Rate+ Cleaning FeeEffective $/Night
1 night $155 $75 $230/night
2 nights $310 $75 $192/night
3 nights $465 $75 $180/night
7 nights $1,085 $75 $166/night
28 nights $4,340 $75 $158/night

This is before Airbnb adds their guest service fee (typically 14%). So a guest booking 2 nights really sees about $219/night all-in.

When to Revisit the Cleaning Fee

If we're getting lots of views but very few bookings on short stays (1–2 nights), our total price might be scaring people off. In that case, we could lower the cleaning fee to $50 and bump the base nightly rate up by $10 to compensate. This "hides" more of the cost in the nightly rate, which looks better in search results.

But don't do this unless we're actually seeing a problem — $75 is standard for DC.

Key takeaway: The prices you see in the Pricing Calendar are nightly rates only. The guest pays that plus a $75 cleaning fee plus Airbnb's service fee. PriceLabs already knows about our cleaning fee and factors it into its pricing decisions — so you don't need to do any extra math.

Your Daily Routine

This should take about 5–10 minutes. Most days, there's nothing to do.

Every Day (5 minutes)

  • Check Airbnb messages — Reply to every guest inquiry within 1 hour if possible. Our response rate needs to stay above 90% for Superhost. Set phone notifications so you don't miss them.
  • Accept or decline booking requests — Accept as many as possible, especially in the early months. Declining hurts our search ranking.
  • Glance at incoming bookings — If a new booking comes in, just note the dates and rate. Does the rate look right for that time period? If it looks way too low for a peak date, it might mean we need to add an override.

On Guest Check-In Days

  • Send the check-in message — 1 hour after check-in time, send: "Hi [Name]! Hope you arrived safely. WiFi password is [password], TV remote is on the coffee table. Let me know if anything is unclear!"

On Day 2 of a Guest Stay

  • Send the mid-stay check-in — "Hi [Name]! Just checking in — is everything going well? Anything I can help with? Enjoy DC!" This little touch leads to better reviews.

On Guest Check-Out Days

  • Send the checkout message — "Hope you had a wonderful stay! If you enjoyed it, a review would mean the world to us." Reviews are our lifeblood.
  • Leave a review for the guest — Even a short one. Guests see you reviewed them and are more likely to review you back.
What you DON'T need to do daily: You don't need to check PriceLabs, adjust prices, or update the Command Center every day. PriceLabs handles pricing automatically. Your daily job is just guest communication.

Your Monthly Routine

Do this on the 1st of every month. Takes about 30–45 minutes.

Step 1: Pull Last Month's Numbers (10 min)

a

Log into Airbnb → Earnings → Transaction History. Set the date range to last month (e.g., March 1–31). Note the total gross revenue and total net payout.

b

Count the number of booked nights from the calendar. Divide by 30 (or 31) to get your occupancy rate. Example: 21 booked nights ÷ 30 = 70%.

c

Divide gross revenue by booked nights to get your average daily rate (ADR). Example: $3,200 ÷ 21 nights = $152/night.

Step 2: Review the Scorecard (5 min)

Compare last month's numbers to these targets:

MetricTargetWhat to Do if Off
Revenue $3,800/month If below $2,500, our base price or occupancy is too low
Occupancy 70%+ Below 55%? Drop base price by $10. Above 85%? Raise it by $10.
ADR $155+ If below $140, our prices are too low — check if events were underpriced
Rating 4.8+ Read recent reviews for patterns. If anything dips, fix that issue immediately

Step 3: Check Upcoming Events (10 min)

Look at the next 60 days. Are there any big DC events coming up? Check the Pricing Calendar tab in this guide, and also check washington.org for newly announced events.

For each big event, make sure there's a date-specific override in PriceLabs with the right price. If there isn't one, add it:

PriceLabs → your listing → Pricing Customizations → Date-Specific Overrides → Add the dates and the price from our calendar.

Step 4: Adjust Base Price If Needed (5 min)

This is the single most impactful thing you can do each month. Based on last month's occupancy:

If Occupancy Was…Do This
Below 55% Lower the base price by $5–10 in PriceLabs
55–75% Keep the base price where it is — we're in a healthy range
Above 80% Raise the base price by $5–10 — we're probably underpriced

To change it: PriceLabs → your listing → Base Price → type the new number → Save.

Step 5: Update the Command Center (10 min)

Open command_center.html in a text editor (like TextEdit on Mac — right-click → Open With → TextEdit). Near the top of the code, you'll see a section called MONTHLY_DATA. Here's exactly what to update:

What to UpdateWhere to Get ItExample
paid Airbnb → Earnings → total net payout for the month 1856
upcoming Set to 0 once the month is over (it was for future projections) 0
total Same as "paid" for completed months 1856

You'll also see a section called MONTHLY_DETAILS — add a row for the month with:

FieldWhere to Get It
adrGross revenue ÷ booked nights (you calculated this in Step 1)
occupancyBooked nights ÷ days in month, as a decimal (e.g., 0.70 for 70%)
bookingsCount of separate reservations that month
ratingYour current overall rating on Airbnb

That's it — just those numbers. The dashboard calculates everything else (charts, trends, comparisons) automatically from this data. If you're not comfortable editing the file, just write the numbers down and Cesar can plug them in — it takes 2 minutes.

Step 6: Quick Listing Refresh (5 min)

  • Update the listing description to mention the current season or upcoming events ("Stay during Capital Pride — walk to the parade!")
  • Rearrange photos if needed — the first photo drives the most clicks
  • Respond to any reviews you haven't replied to yet
That's it! Six steps, once a month. The most important ones are Steps 3 (event overrides) and 4 (base price adjustment). Everything else is bonus polish.

Are the Prices Too High? When to Pivot

This is the most important question. Here's a clear framework so you always know whether to stay the course or adjust.

First: Why the Prices Feel High

If you're used to seeing us charge $133–150/night for everything, then yes — $200+ on a Friday or $320 for July 4th feels like a lot. But here's the thing: we were undercharging. The DC median for a 1-bedroom is $146/night, and that's the average — it includes slow Tuesdays in January. Weekend rates and event rates in DC routinely hit $200–300 for comparable listings.

Our 5.0 rating across 64 reviews puts us in the top tier of DC listings. We shouldn't be pricing like a budget option.

That said, if the market tells us we're wrong, we listen. Here's how to read the signals.

The 30-Day Test

Give the new prices at least 30 full days before drawing conclusions. Why? Because Airbnb bookings don't happen instantly — most guests book 2–4 weeks ahead. If we raise prices today, the bookings (or lack thereof) from that change won't show up for several weeks.

Here's the timeline:

WhenWhat to Look AtWhat's Normal
Days 1–14 Views and saved listings on Airbnb Views might dip slightly — that's OK. Airbnb sometimes shows us less while adjusting to new prices. Don't panic.
Days 15–30 New bookings for 2–6 weeks out This is where you start to see real signal. Are bookings still coming in? Even 2–3 new bookings in this window is healthy.
Day 30 Occupancy for the next 30 days This is your verdict. Check the number below.

The Pivot Signals — When to Lower Prices

Lower the base price by $10 if any of these are true after 30 days:

SignalThresholdWhat to Do
Occupancy for next 30 days Below 40% Drop base price by $10 in PriceLabs. Wait another 14 days. If still below 40%, drop another $10.
Zero new bookings 14+ days with nothing Check if it's a seasonal thing (Jan/Feb are naturally slow). If it's April–October and we're getting nothing, prices are likely too high.
Lots of views, no bookings 200+ views, 0–1 bookings in 30 days People are looking but not buying. Price is the likely culprit. Drop base price by $5–10 or lower the cleaning fee to $50.

The Stay-the-Course Signals

Keep prices where they are (or even raise them!) if you see:

SignalWhat It Means
Occupancy above 55% for next 30 days Healthy. Guests are booking at our higher prices. The strategy is working.
Occupancy above 80% We're actually too cheap. Raise the base price $10. If everything fills, we left money on the table.
Bookings coming in 3–6 weeks ahead Advance bookings mean confidence in our listing. People plan around our price — great sign.
Higher revenue even with fewer nights booked This is the goal. 18 nights at $180/night ($3,240) beats 25 nights at $135/night ($3,375) because there's less cleaning, less wear, and less work — and the numbers are similar.

The Quick-Check Cheat Sheet

Open the Airbnb app, go to your calendar, and count booked nights in the next 30 days:

Booked Nights (Next 30 Days)VerdictAction
0–8 nights (under 27%) Trouble Drop base price $10, check if dates have overrides that are too high
9–15 nights (30–50%) Watch Give it one more week. If no improvement, drop by $5
16–21 nights (53–70%) Healthy Stay the course — this is right where we want to be
22+ nights (73%+) Raise prices We're underpriced. Bump base by $5–10.
Remember: PriceLabs is already handling the daily adjustments. Your job is just the monthly gut check: look at next-30-day occupancy, and nudge the base price $5–10 in the right direction. Small moves, not big swings. And always give changes at least 2 weeks to take effect before changing again.

Quick Glossary

Terms you'll see in PriceLabs, on Airbnb, and in the Command Center.

TermWhat It Means
ADR Average Daily Rate — the average price per night we actually received. Total revenue ÷ booked nights.
Occupancy Rate The percentage of available nights that were booked. 21 booked nights out of 30 = 70% occupancy.
Base Price The starting point PriceLabs uses. It adjusts up or down from this number. Think of it as your "normal Tuesday night" price.
Override A price you manually lock in for specific dates. PriceLabs won't go below this. Used for big events.
Min Stay The minimum number of nights a guest must book. Set higher during peak events to avoid short stays taking up prime dates.
Orphan Night A lonely 1–2 night gap between two bookings. Hard to fill, so we discount them.
Seasonal Profile PriceLabs settings that adjust your base price for each month/season. April might be +40%, January might be -15%.
Superhost Airbnb badge for top hosts. Requires: 4.8+ rating, 90% response rate, 10+ stays/year, less than 1% cancellations. We currently have it!
Instant Book Lets guests book without you having to manually approve. Faster bookings = higher search ranking.
RevPAN Revenue Per Available Night — total revenue ÷ total nights in the month (booked or not). A better measure than ADR because it accounts for empty nights.
Pacing How quickly bookings are coming in compared to the same time last month/year. If May is 60% booked and it's only April 10, pacing is strong.
Comp Set Comparable listings in our area — what we compete against. PriceLabs looks at these to set our prices.
